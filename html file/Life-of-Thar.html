LIFE IN THE THAR DESERT

The Thar Desert is one of Pakistan's most culturally distinct and vivid areas. Located in Sindh province, it is populated
by both Muslim and Hindu communities, who have a long history of mutual respect and living in harmony together.
Muslims in Thar avoid eating cow meat out of respect to the Hindu population; and during the month of Ramadan,
Hindus do not eat in front of their fasting Muslim neighbours. Both communities have been affected by prolonged
drought in the region.

IN THE GRIP OF DROUGHT

Historically, the Tharis have been agro-pastoralists. But this livelihood is under threat as the region has seen eight droughts in the last 60 years. The current drought began in 2013 and with every passing month, it increases its vice-like grip on the community. Soaring temperatures sometimes exceed 50°C.

"THERE ARE NO CROPS AND OUR CATTLE ARE DYING".

Sawautri, 37 years old, is married with five children – three daughters and two sons. Sawautri says “There is drought in our area for four years now. There are no crops and our cattle are dying. We sell our animals for half price and make ends meet with that”..

There is simply not enough food and water to prevent their cattle from dying. Her husband Lajpat has to travel to the major cities, Karachi and Hyderabad, to find work as an unskilled labourer. He earns an average of 6000 PKR a month – the equivalent of approximately €54. Sawautri volunteers as a community health worker with one of our partner organisations and earns a stipend of 2000 PKR /€18 per month.

Their younger son had suffered from malnutrition but thankfully, he received treatment through Concern supported interventions in their village and has made a full recovery.

THE STRUGGLE TO SUSTAIN LIFE

The people of Thar have devised many different ways of sustaining life in their desert home, some of which have lasting consequences on their lives, such as selling of livestock at low prices, or accumulating debt that can take years to repay. THE DEVASTATING IMPACT OF STARVATION

The impact of slow starvation takes many forms. As the body weakens, people become more susceptible to becoming sick. Diseases such as diarrhoea are common in Thar because access to clean drinking water is difficult even at the best of times. The worst affected are pregnant and lactating women and their young children, where the deprivation of adequate food quickly leads to malnourishment, and even death.
